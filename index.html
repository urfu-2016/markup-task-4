Bond

Not since Robin Hood has there been such a spectac­ular and flercely patri­ot­ic folk hero as James Bond. Since his dash­ing debut in 1953 in the book "Casino Royale" he has emphat­ically become the most awesome secret agent in the world. A lonewolf locensed to kill and la­dykill he has also made an­oth­er kind of killing in the cin­ema where he is undis­puted king of the box-office.

A sort of one-main export over drive, the first three Bond pic­tures have now been seen by more than 100 million people around the world and earned a massive $75 million. Now Bond IV - "Thunder-ball" – has opened. Almost as inevitable as Bond's ul­ti­mate triumph over such powerful evils as Smersh and Spectre. movie records will fall to this film.

Envied

Bond is a veri­ta­ble queue-making ma­ch­ine. Admired, envied, despired, hated, aped, and attacked? Ian Flem­ing's hero is pos­itively the most discussed and compulsive invention of our time. But exactly what is the iresis­tible appeal of this man called Bond? There have been oth­er spies, oth­er heroes, oth­er special agents – think of Dick Barton, Bulldog Drummond, nero Wolfe, and Chan­dler's mem­o­rable Philip Marlowe – but none has achieved the same pas­sion­ate popular­ity as 007. Yet certainly Drummond could match his nick-of-timing, Wolfe is in the same sybarit­ic league and Chan­dler certainly had more razored dia­logue. So what precisely does Bond have that somehow eluded the rest? Ba­sically it is a cunning com­pound of ism. An amalgam of such raw ingre­di­ents as sexualism, sadism, cyn­icism, witticism. You might also throw in eroticism, pro­fes­sion­alism, magnetism, and even patri­o­tism. (It is notewor­thy that not a single villain in Bond's escapades is British. Russians, Germans, Kore­ans, but nev­er British.)

Villain

The villain in "Thundeball" is one Largo, an Itelian with black eye patch and ghost-white hair. And it is in this film that the essentialisms of Bond can be observed in action. The story is not 15 minutes old before we are treated to a soupcon, of sensu­alism in mink gloves, a Jet-Pack of escapism and a whole load of vintage Bond cyn­icism. Now read on: The initials of the cof­fin drape were J.B. James Bond watched the fu­neral ser­vice from the gallery, a rather more comfort­able po­sition than the occupant of the oak cas­ket. The woman by his side, Mademoi­selle La Porte, said soft­ly: "At least you've been saved the effort of removing him. Colonel Bouvar passed away in his sleep, I'm told." The sadness of Bond;s face was not the sadness of the be­reaved. "You look disap­pointed you did not kill him your­self?" said Mademoi­selle La Porte. "I am, Jacques Bouvar murdered two of my col­leagues," said Bond. Out­side the small church just out­side Paris, he watched the wid­ow Bouvar open the door of the black li­mou­sine and climb in. Some­thing hap­pened in Bond's face that was suddenly close to pleasure. The wid­ow Bouvar's mourning would be la­mentably short. Bond knew he would kill her When she arrived at her chateau James Bond was wait­ing for her. "Madame, I have come to offer my sin­cere con­do­lences," he said as his fist sma­shed through her black veil. "I don't think you should have ope­ned that car door by your­self my dear Colonel Bouvar."

Violent

Bouvar, his veil and wig torn aside to re­veal the hideous make up on his hard face, climbed to his feet. The fight was good and Bouver died vio­lently, stran­gled with an iron poker. Thi sudden sav­age sadism ended – and note the pro­fes­sion­alism with which it was accomplished – James Bond makes his 1966 get­away. No wait­ing, revved-up car for him. For james Bond has tak­en the hero into the mir­acle of the Space Age. Marlowe and Wolfe and dear old Dick Barton were finally caught in a world of street-corner re­alism, born at a time when 90 on the clock meant a fast car.They were for ev­er earth­bound by convention.

Fantasy

Bond has bro­ken away, tak­en the fanta­sy of the com­ic strip and giv­en it a pop art re­ality. Hilar­ious yet odd­ly logical. It is part of his extraor­dinary cunning of mixing excapism with a sence of fast approach­ing re­alism that Bond is now. So: having disman­tled the life of Colonel Bouvar Bond straps on his U.S. Jet-Pack and simply flies away. "No well-dressed man should be with­out one," he murmurs. So far, so fast. But even James Bond sometimes must rest. He re­treats to a health farm where the farm­ers are nat­urally very young very pretty, and very fem­i­nine. Pat is the masseuse with the mostest–including a mink glove to soothe away Mr. Bond's ten­sions. This of course is the brand of medicinal sensu­alism that James Bond thrives on. He is soon on his feet again. But even a country health farm is not a haven of rest when Bond is about. Strapped to a motorised traction table, an intru­ment for stretch­ing the spine. Bond is almost stretched from here to eter­nity when a villain switches it to maximum power. "Somebody," grits Bond when freed, "is go­ing to pay for this."
